The Internet Is Dying-And It's Being Killed on Purpose

What if most of what you see online wasn't made by people anymore? What if the memes, videos, comments-even the arguments-were all generated by machines, optimized to keep you scrolling, not thinking? Welcome to the age of slop capitalism, where the internet is no longer a place for human connection, but a factory of algorithmic noise.

It sounds like a conspiracy theory. But it's not. It's happening right now, and the companies behind it aren't hiding it. In fact, they're proud of it.

What Is Slop Capitalism?

Slop capitalism is a term coined to describe the growing trend of platforms replacing human-generated content with cheap, AI-generated alternatives. The goal? Maximize engagement while minimizing costs. Why pay creators when you can flood feeds with endless, low-effort content that keeps users hooked?

Think of it like fast food for the mind. It's cheap, addictive, and everywhere. But instead of burgers, it's AI-generated videos of LeBron James milking a cow or kissing baby Shrek. These surreal, often nonsensical clips rack up millions of views-not because they're meaningful, but because they're engineered to hijack your attention.

Meta, the parent company of Facebook and Instagram, has quietly shifted its strategy to promote this kind of content. AI accounts are now being incentivized. Human creators are being pushed out. And the result is a feed that feels increasingly artificial, even uncanny.

The Dead Internet Theory: From Fringe to Fact

The Dead Internet Theory once sounded like digital paranoia. It claimed that much of the internet was already run by bots, with real human interaction becoming the exception rather than the rule. But in 2024, that theory is starting to look less like fiction and more like a business model.

Meta's Vice President of Generative AI, Chris Hayes, recently confirmed that AI personas will soon exist on their platforms just like regular users. These bots will have names, bios, profile pictures-and they'll post content, comment, and interact. In other words, they'll pretend to be people.

And they're not alone. Spotify has been caught stuffing playlists with AI-generated music to avoid paying artists. Google's new "AI Overview" feature summarizes search results, replacing links to real websites with ad-friendly blurbs. The pattern is clear: replace humans, reduce costs, increase control.

Why It Matters

This isn't just about weird memes or fake music. It's about the erosion of human expression online. Every AI-generated post that goes viral is one less opportunity for a real person to be heard. Every bot that comments on your post is one less chance for genuine connection.

And it's not just harmless filler. Some of the most viral AI content is deeply problematic. Racist, sexist, and offensive videos are being algorithmically promoted because they drive engagement. The more outrageous the content, the more people react-and the more money platforms make.

In one disturbing example, AI-generated videos featuring racist stereotypes of Black men racked up tens of millions of views. These weren't fringe posts. They were algorithmically boosted, part of a system that rewards spectacle over substance, division over dialogue.

The Algorithmic Gaze

At the heart of slop capitalism is what media theorists call the "algorithmic gaze." It's the idea that platforms no longer care about what content means-only how well it performs. The message doesn't matter. The metrics do.

This gaze flattens everything into "content." A heartfelt story, a political rant, a dancing cat, a racist meme-they're all just data points in a feed. The more you engage, the more you get. And the more you get, the less you think.

It's a system designed not to inform or inspire, but to distract and extract. Your attention is the product. And slop is the delivery method.

What Can We Do?

There's no easy fix. But there are ways to resist. Start by being intentional with your time online. Seek out human creators. Support independent media. Diversify your information sources. And most importantly, recognize that the platforms themselves are not neutral-they are shaping what you see, think, and feel.

Slop capitalism thrives on passivity. It wants you to scroll, not question. To consume, not connect. But the internet doesn't have to be this way. It was built by people. It can still be reclaimed by people.

Maybe the most radical thing you can do today is to log off, call a friend, and have a real conversation. Because in a world of bots pretending to be people, being human is the most subversive act of all.
