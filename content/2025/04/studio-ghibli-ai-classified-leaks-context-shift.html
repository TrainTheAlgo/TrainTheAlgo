Context Is the New Content

What if the real power in today's world isn't in what's said, but how it's said? In a digital landscape flooded with AI-generated art, political leaks, and economic policy shifts, the framing of information now shapes reality more than the facts themselves. From Studio Ghibli-style AI filters to national security breaches on Signal, the context in which we consume content is becoming the true driver of influence, value, and perception.

The Ghibli Filter and the Illusion of Art

It started with a tweet. A user posted AI-generated images of himself and his wife transformed into characters from a Studio Ghibli film. The internet exploded. Thousands followed suit, uploading their own Ghibli-fied pets, partners, and profile pictures. It was cute, viral, and seemingly harmless.

But beneath the surface, something deeper was happening. The style being mimicked belongs to Hayao Miyazaki, the legendary animator behind Ghibli classics like Spirited Away and My Neighbor Totoro. Miyazaki has been outspoken in his disdain for AI-generated art, calling it "an insult to life itself." Yet here was his aesthetic, repackaged by algorithms, stripped of its soul, and turned into a social media trend.

This wasn't about the art. It was about the context. The images themselves were disposable. What mattered was who shared them, how they were framed, and the dopamine rush of likes and retweets. The platforms captured the value. The artist captured none.

Leaks, Lies, and the Signal Chat Debacle

While Ghibli AI was trending, a very different kind of story was unfolding. Senior Trump administration officials were caught discussing military plans for Yemen in a Signal group chat. One of them accidentally added a journalist. The leak wasn't just a breach of protocol-it was a masterclass in how context can overshadow content.

The actual content of the messages-potentially violating the Espionage Act-was serious. But the public conversation focused on the absurdity of the leak itself: the use of a commercial app, the accidental invite, the denials. The framing became the story. The substance got buried.

In a world where war plans and memes share the same digital space, our brains struggle to assign appropriate weight. The platform becomes the lens through which we interpret everything. And that lens is often warped.

Tariffs, Trade, and the Theater of Policy

Consider Trump's recent announcement of a 25% tariff on countries buying Venezuelan oil. The economic implications were real, but the market's reaction was driven more by what the move signaled than what it actually did. It wasn't about the policy-it was about the posture.

In today's hyper-connected world, policy is performance. The content of a decision matters less than the narrative it creates. Investors, allies, and voters respond to the frame, not the facts. And that frame is increasingly shaped by social media, influencers, and AI-generated spin.

AI Isn't Just Replacing Jobs-It's Rewriting Meaning

We've long worried about AI taking over tasks. But the real shift is more profound. AI is now reshaping how meaning is made. It's not just automating labor-it's automating culture. The Ghibli AI trend is a perfect example. The images are technically impressive, but they lack the emotional depth of Miyazaki's originals. They're aesthetic, not art.

Art is a process. It's messy, personal, and often painful. AI-generated content, by contrast, is clean, fast, and frictionless. But when everyone can create something that looks like art, the value shifts from the creation to the curation. The frame becomes the product.

The Rise of Conspicuous Contextualization

Thorstein Veblen coined the term "conspicuous consumption" to describe buying things for status, not utility. Today, we're seeing a new version: conspicuous contextualization. People don't just share content-they share it in ways that signal identity, values, and belonging.

Posting a Ghibli AI image isn't about the image. It's about being part of the trend. It's about saying, "I'm in the know." The same goes for political takes, economic hot takes, and viral memes. The content is secondary. The context is everything.

Platforms Profit, Creators Lose

This shift has massive economic implications. Platforms like Twitter and TikTok monetize engagement. AI companies harvest data. But the original creators-whether they're artists, authors, or policymakers-often see little return. The value is extracted from the context, not the content.

Studio Ghibli saw a spike in interest during the AI trend, but it didn't translate into revenue. Meanwhile, AI developers trained their models on millions of user-submitted images. The users got likes. The platforms got profits. The artist got nothing.

Why Context Literacy Matters

In finance, we regulate how investment opportunities are presented. We understand that framing can mislead. But in the information economy, we're still playing catch-up. Most regulation focuses on content-what's true or false-not on how that content is framed and distributed.

We need a new kind of literacy. Context literacy. The ability to recognize how framing shapes perception. The ability to question not just what we're seeing, but how and why we're seeing it. This isn't just a media issue-it's a democratic one. When context becomes the battleground, those who control the frame control the future.

The Medium Is Still the Message

Marshall McLuhan said it decades ago: the medium is the message. Today, that insight feels more relevant than ever. Whether it's a Ghibli-style selfie, a leaked war plan, or a new economic policy, the platform and the frame often matter more than the substance.

We're living in a world where AI can generate infinite content, but meaning is scarce. And meaning is made in context. The question isn't just what's being said-it's who's saying it, where, and why. That's the real story. That's the real power.

And maybe, just maybe, the next revolution won't be about what we create-but how we choose to see it.
