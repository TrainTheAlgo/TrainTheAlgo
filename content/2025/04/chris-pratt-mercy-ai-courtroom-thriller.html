When AI Becomes the Judge

What happens when the justice system is no longer human? In Mercy, Chris Pratt finds out the hard way. Set in a near-future courtroom run entirely by artificial intelligence, this upcoming thriller promises to be more than just a movie-it's a mirror held up to our rapidly evolving relationship with technology.

Announced at CinemaCon on April 3, 2025, Mercy is already generating buzz for its bold concept and high-stakes storytelling. The film is slated for release on January 23, 2026, and stars Pratt as a detective accused of murdering his wife. The twist? He's being judged by an AI system he helped build.

90 Minutes to Prove Innocence

The plot unfolds in real time. Pratt's character is strapped to a chair, confined to a sterile courtroom where the judge is not a person, but a machine. Voiced by Rebecca Ferguson, the AI judge processes evidence, testimony, and emotional cues with chilling precision. The detective has just 90 minutes to prove his innocence before the system delivers a final, irreversible verdict.

This isn't just a sci-fi gimmick. The film taps into real-world debates about the use of AI in legal systems. Courts around the globe are already experimenting with machine learning to assist in sentencing, bail decisions, and case prioritization. In 2024 alone, over 100 million cases were processed worldwide, with some regions facing delays of up to 18 months. AI, proponents argue, could help clear these backlogs and reduce human bias.

But Mercy doesn't let the technology off the hook. It asks the uncomfortable questions: Can a machine truly understand context? What happens when the system gets it wrong? And who is accountable when justice is automated?

Chris Pratt's Personal Touch

At CinemaCon, Pratt leaned into the film's intensity with a theatrical stunt. He appeared on stage strapped to the same chair used in the movie, challenging himself to thank exhibitors within a strict time limit. If he failed, he joked, he'd face a penalty-mirroring the pressure his character faces in the film. The moment was lighthearted, but it underscored the film's central tension: the ticking clock, the inescapable system, the fight for truth.

Pratt's involvement goes beyond acting. He reportedly collaborated closely with the creative team to shape the story's emotional core. His character isn't just battling an AI judge-he's confronting the consequences of his own creation. That personal stake adds depth to the narrative, making it more than just a thriller. It's a cautionary tale about innovation without foresight.

The Voice of Judgment

Rebecca Ferguson's role as the AI judge adds another layer of intrigue. Her voice, modulated to reflect the system's cold logic, is both soothing and unsettling. It's not the voice of a villain, but of a system that believes it's doing the right thing. That ambiguity is what makes the film so compelling. The AI isn't evil-it's efficient. And that might be even more dangerous.

While the director's name remains under wraps, insiders describe the vision as a blend of Minority Report and 12 Angry Men, with a dash of Ex Machina. The production is said to use cutting-edge visual effects to bring the AI courtroom to life, creating a sterile, high-tech environment that contrasts sharply with the raw emotion of Pratt's performance.

Why It Matters

Mercy arrives at a time when society is grappling with the ethical boundaries of AI. From ChatGPT in classrooms to algorithms in hiring and policing, the line between human and machine judgment is blurring. This film doesn't just entertain-it provokes. It forces viewers to consider what we're willing to delegate to machines, and what must remain human.

As AI continues to shape our world, stories like Mercy become essential. They help us imagine the future before it arrives. They challenge us to think critically about the systems we build. And sometimes, they remind us that the most powerful courtroom isn't made of code or concrete-it's the one inside our conscience.
