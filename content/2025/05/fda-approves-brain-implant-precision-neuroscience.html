FDA Clears Precision Neuroscience's Brain Implant for Human Trials

What if a thin film placed on your brain could help you move a cursor, type a message, or even regain lost mobility? That's no longer science fiction. On May 10, 2025, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration gave Precision Neuroscience the green light to begin human trials of its brain-computer interface (BCI) system-a breakthrough that could redefine how we treat paralysis and neurological disorders.

A New Kind of Brain Implant

Unlike traditional brain implants that require deep surgical insertion, Precision's system uses a wafer-thin, flexible film embedded with microelectrodes. This film is placed on the surface of the brain, not inside it. The goal? To capture high-resolution neural signals without the risks of penetrating brain tissue.

This approach is designed to be safer and more accessible. It avoids the complications associated with more invasive procedures, while still offering the precision needed to decode brain activity. The system translates these signals into commands that can control external devices-like a computer cursor or robotic limb-offering new hope to people with conditions like ALS or spinal cord injuries.

How It Works

Think of the brain as a symphony of electrical signals. Precision's implant listens to that symphony from the surface, capturing the patterns that correspond to movement, intention, or speech. These signals are then decoded by software and translated into digital commands. The result is a direct communication line between the brain and a device-no muscle movement required.

In preclinical trials, the system demonstrated the ability to record and interpret these signals with remarkable accuracy. The FDA's clearance now allows the company to test the technology in humans, with trials expected to begin by the end of 2025.

The Competitive Landscape

Precision Neuroscience isn't alone in this race. Neuralink, founded by Elon Musk, has already implanted its N1 chip in three patients. Synchron, another U.S.-based company, has implanted its stent-like device in ten patients globally. Each company is taking a different path to the same goal: restoring autonomy to people with severe disabilities.

Neuralink's approach involves inserting electrodes directly into the brain, offering high signal fidelity but with greater surgical risk. Synchron's device is inserted through blood vessels, making it less invasive but potentially limited in signal quality. Precision's surface-based system aims to strike a balance-minimally invasive, yet capable of capturing detailed neural data.

Why This Matters

For patients with locked-in syndrome, ALS, or spinal cord injuries, BCIs could be life-changing. Imagine being able to communicate again, control a wheelchair, or interact with a computer using only your thoughts. That's the promise of this technology.

Dr. Anna Wexler, a neuroethics expert at the University of Pennsylvania, sees the potential but urges caution. "Minimally invasive systems like Precision's could lower surgical risks, but we need robust data on durability and equitable access," she said. Meanwhile, Dr. Michael Okun, a neurologist at the University of Florida, believes competition is key: "Multiple players innovating in BCI push the field forward, ensuring diverse solutions for patients."

What's Next?

Precision Neuroscience plans to begin trials with a small group of patients, focusing on safety and the system's ability to translate brain signals into real-world actions. If successful, the company aims to expand to 50 patients by 2026. Backed by $200 million in funding from investors like BlackRock and T. Rowe Price, the company is well-positioned to scale quickly.

The FDA's clearance also signals growing regulatory support for BCI innovation. In 2024, the agency approved Neuralink's implants for speech and vision restoration. Now, with Precision's system entering human trials, the momentum in neurotech is undeniable.

The Bigger Picture

Brain-computer interfaces are no longer confined to labs or sci-fi novels. They're entering hospitals, clinical trials, and-soon-real lives. The technology is still young, and challenges remain. Long-term safety, affordability, and ethical concerns must be addressed. But the direction is clear.

We're moving toward a future where the brain can speak directly to machines, bypassing broken nerves or damaged muscles. For millions living with neurological conditions, that future can't come soon enough.

Sometimes, the most powerful connections are the ones you can't see-but can finally feel again.
