The Return of Speed

What if you could fly from New York to Tokyo in under five hours-and pay less than a thousand dollars for the ticket? That's not science fiction. It's the mission of a small team in San Francisco who believe the world is overdue for a speed upgrade.

Astro Mechanica, a startup you've probably never heard of, is building a supersonic jet that could change how we think about distance. Their secret weapon? A turboelectric adaptive engine that morphs mid-flight, running on liquefied natural gas. It's fast, clean, and radically different from anything flying today.

Meanwhile, in a quieter corner of the news cycle, something unexpected is happening at the bottom of the world. Antarctica's ice sheet, long a symbol of climate crisis, has grown by 108 gigatons per year from 2021 to 2023. The data comes from NASA satellites and Chinese researchers. The story barely made a ripple.

Faster Than the Speed of Bureaucracy

Astro Mechanica isn't just building a jet. They're rebuilding the aviation industry from scratch. Founder Ian Brooke and his team of fewer than ten engineers are doing what Boeing hasn't done in decades-innovate.

While Boeing assembles parts from dozens of suppliers, Astro is designing everything around a single idea: speed, without compromise. Their engine is part jet, part electric fan, part rocket. It adapts in real time, switching modes for takeoff, cruising, and supersonic flight. It's like a transmission for the sky.

And it runs on liquefied natural gas. LNG burns cleaner than kerosene, offers more range, and costs a fraction of the price. It can even be made from air, water, and sunlight. That's not just good for the planet-it's good business.

From Luxury to Mass Market

Supersonic travel used to be a luxury for the elite. The Concorde was fast but inefficient, noisy, and expensive. It never evolved. When it retired in 2003, the dream of fast flight went with it.

Astro Mechanica wants to bring it back-and make it affordable. Think Southwest prices, not private jet premiums. Their model is Uber for the skies: small jets, on-demand flights, and regional airports that skip the chaos of major hubs.

It's not just about speed. It's about access. When travel time drops, the world gets smaller. A business meeting in Tokyo and dinner in San Francisco? Possible. A weekend in Sydney? Why not.

Climate Clarity

While Astro is shrinking the world, Antarctica is quietly growing. From 2021 to 2023, the ice sheet added 108 gigatons per year. That's a reversal of the previous decade's trend, where it lost 142 gigatons annually.

The cause? Increased precipitation. More snow means more ice. It's likely a short-term anomaly, not a long-term shift. But it's real, and it matters. Yet most media outlets ignored the story. Only the New York Post covered it.

This isn't about denying climate change. It's about understanding it fully. The planet is complex. Trends fluctuate. And sometimes, the data surprises us. That's why it's crucial to look at the whole picture-not just the parts that fit a narrative.

Progress Isn't Inevitable

We flew faster than sound before we had smartphones. Then we stopped. The Concorde sits in museums, a relic of ambition. But ambition is back. And it's wearing jeans, not suits.

Astro Mechanica's team is small, scrappy, and fast. They've already built and tested three generations of their engine. In the time it takes Boeing to redesign a tray table, Astro has gone from concept to hot-fire testing.

They just raised $27 million to build the jet. The engine works. The vision is clear. Now it's airplane time.

Why It Matters

Speed changes everything. When you can get anywhere in a few hours, your options multiply. New markets open. New relationships form. New ideas spread. Supersonic travel is broadband for bodies.

And just like broadband, it won't just shift market share-it will grow the market. When Uber launched, it didn't just take riders from taxis. It created new demand. Supersonic flight could do the same for global travel.

Meanwhile, the climate conversation needs more nuance. Yes, the planet is warming. But not every data point fits the apocalypse narrative. The Antarctic rebound is a reminder to stay curious, not just concerned.

We're entering a new era-faster, cleaner, and more connected. The world is shrinking again. And this time, everyone gets a seat.

What would you do if the world was three times closer?
