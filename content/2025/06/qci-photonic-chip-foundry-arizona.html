Quantum Leap: A New Era for Photonic Chips Begins in Arizona

What if the future of quantum computing didn't rely on electrons, but on light? That's the bet Quantum Computing Inc. (QCi) is making with its newly launched photonic chip foundry in Tempe, Arizona. And if they're right, this facility could mark a turning point in the race to build practical, scalable quantum machines.

On June 4, 2025, QCi officially announced the operational launch of its state-of-the-art thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN) photonic chip foundry. The ribbon-cutting ceremony, held on May 12, wasn't just a photo op-it was a signal that the company is ready to move from research to real-world impact. The foundry is designed to produce high-performance photonic chips that use light instead of electricity to process quantum information. This approach could solve some of the biggest challenges in quantum computing today.

Why Photonic Chips Matter

Quantum computers promise to solve problems that are impossible for classical machines. But building a stable, scalable quantum system is notoriously difficult. Most current systems rely on superconducting or trapped ion qubits, which are sensitive to noise and require extreme cooling. Photonic chips, by contrast, use photons-particles of light-to represent qubits. They're less prone to decoherence, can operate at room temperature, and are easier to integrate with existing fiber-optic infrastructure.

QCi's chips are built using TFLN, a material known for its high electro-optic efficiency and low optical loss. This makes it ideal for creating fast, low-power photonic circuits. The Tempe facility is one of the first in the U.S. dedicated to manufacturing these chips at scale, giving QCi a strategic edge in a field that's heating up fast.

From Lab to Market

Dr. William McGann, QCi's CEO, sees the foundry as a critical step toward commercializing quantum technology. "This facility positions us to deliver accessible and affordable quantum solutions to global markets," he said during the launch. The chips produced here will support QCi's own quantum systems and be available to external customers looking for U.S.-based photonic chip manufacturing.

That's a big deal. Most photonic chip production today happens overseas, and supply chain concerns have made domestic manufacturing a priority. By building in Arizona, QCi is not only boosting its own capabilities but also contributing to national efforts to secure quantum technology leadership.

Economic and Strategic Impact

The foundry is expected to create over 100 high-tech jobs in the region, from engineers and technicians to materials scientists and quantum physicists. Local government officials have backed the project, citing its potential to turn Tempe into a hub for quantum innovation. The facility also strengthens the U.S. position in a global race where China, Europe, and tech giants like Google and Microsoft are all investing heavily.

While QCi hasn't disclosed exact production targets, the foundry is equipped for large-scale output. That means it could become a key supplier not just for quantum computing, but also for emerging applications like quantum networking, secure communications, and advanced sensing.

The Road Ahead

Of course, challenges remain. Photonic quantum computing is still in its early stages, and building fault-tolerant systems is a long-term goal. Critics argue that while photonics offer advantages, they also face hurdles in terms of integration and error correction. But QCi is betting that its vertically integrated approach-designing, fabricating, and deploying its own chips-will help it move faster and more efficiently than competitors.

And they're not alone. A growing number of startups and research labs are exploring photonic architectures, drawn by the promise of faster, more stable quantum systems. But few have the manufacturing muscle that QCi now brings to the table.

In a field where progress is often measured in qubits and coherence times, the launch of a full-scale photonic chip foundry is a rare and tangible milestone. It's a reminder that the future of quantum computing won't just be written in code-it will be etched in light.
